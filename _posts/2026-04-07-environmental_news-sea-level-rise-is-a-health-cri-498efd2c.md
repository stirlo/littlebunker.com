Those facing the earliest and harshest consequences are, overwhelmingly, those who did the least to create themThere are moments in history when a crisis long treated as distant reveals itself to be intimate, immediate and profoundly human. Sea-level rise is one of those moments.For years it has been discussed in the abstract language of centimetres, coastal infrastructure and future projections. This can make it seem like a technical challenge – something for engineers and planners to grapple with. But rising seas are already damaging bodies, minds, livelihoods and cultures. Sea-level rise is a present-day health crisis.Christiana Figueres was the head of the UN climate change convention from 2010 to 2016.
